Credit Card Collections

This site was designed for people trying to understand and navigate the world of debt collections. Millions of people are affected by life events every year that may lead, but is not limited to:

  • Past due credit card bills
  • Past due car loans/notes
  • Eviction
  • Car reposession
  • Dealing with agressive debt collectors

We are all (unless you're really lucky) subject to a collections phone call sometime in our lives. Navigating what to do next can be tricky. I have outlined some possible steps to take to help get one back on track.

What is a creditor?

A creditor is the bank or the company that has agreed to lend you money. Normally, an application for credit is filled out and submitted by you, the debtor. The bank then reviews your credit application and determines based on your credit worthiness, to lend you (or not) a specified amount of money to help you make a purchase. This bank (e.g. Chase, Citibank, Capital One, a local credit union, etc.) then becomes the creditor and will show on your credit report reporting the amount they provided you as a limit, as well as: how much you may owe them (it can be $0), your payment history, credit utilization, historic high balance, etc.

But, what would happen if you owe a balance and you just stopped paying on it or you couldn't afford to pay on it anymore? Welcome to the world of debt collections. You have to understand, if the bank is worth their weight in salt, they wont immediately send you to debt collections. They perform what is called "internal collections." This is typically triggered by allowing your account to go from 1 day to 186 days past due. You will notice that you may start receiving numerous phone calls everyday.

Why so many phone calls?

This is a very common collection tactic. It's not the banks goal to harrass you (that is illegal!) but, to keep this debt fresh in your mind so that when you are paying bills, you will think about them. Seems counter intuitive because the phone calls can very easily be seen as an annoyance, however, this is a successful tactic and that is why it is still employed today. We are all human, we forget things. Especially if you are experiencing a hardship, your life may be busier than the average person's, hence it would be easy to forget about the debt.

How do I stop these phone calls?

Thankfully, we are protected as consumers to prevent harrassment from collection agencies including creditors. To stop these calls, the most effective way requires you to directly talk to the person calling you. In a nice and respectful, but firm tone, simply say "stop calling me." The law requires that they immediately stop. They may still mail you statements and collections correspondence to keep you up to date. However, this shouldn't be the last time you speak with them. You should, at least once a month, contact them and update them on your situation and try to work out a payment plan if you believe you owe the debt. Advising them to stop calling you will not erase your debt with them.

They are asking for way too much! I just can't pay what they are asking for.

Creditors understand this. Even if you think your account is a total loss and there is no way you can make a large payment that is way outside your budget, come up with a number that you know you can pay on a monthly basis. Call the creditor and let them know what you can do. You might be surprised with what they can offer! Dont be offended if you receive a counter offer for a monthly payment. That could be truly the best that they can do at the time. If you are unable to do that then you made need a professional to help you navigate your situation. Luckily, there are plenty of those out there!

Debt Settlement Agency vs Credit Counseling Agency

There seems to be a huge misunderstanding between these two types of companies. While seemingly similar, each has a completely different approach to credit card debt, loans, and even mortgages. Debt settlement agencies work towards just that, debt settlements. You pay them $x amount per account enrolled with them per month and in turn, they save up on your behalf and "negotiate" a settlement with your creditors and take a percentage for themselves. Unfortunately, in most people's situations this requires the creditor to charge-off the debt to a third-party debt collection agency. Something they dont tell you is that they usually never negotiate with creditors, even if the creditor sends a settlement offer. Why? Because they get more money that way by forcing the creditors hand to send it to a third party. Sounds like a win though right? Eh, depends. It usually trashes your credit as they keep the creditor in the dark. Sometimes, a creditor may choose to pursue the debt in court, the debt settlement agency, will not represent you in court. Not to mention the possible tax complications. You're on your own there. When it comes to these companies offering all these nice things about getting you out of your debt and you only paying for a portion of the balance. It may be too good to be true. A TON of research is required to see if they are actually a good fit for you. At which point, you may be better off negotiating a settlement by yourself.

Credit counseling groups on the other hand are very much loved by creditors and are the recommended route by the government. These are typically non-profit organizations that will basically reach out to all your creditors on your behalf and see what they can offer in regards to a payment plan. They then report back to you to let you know what each creditor is willing to do, add up all the payments, add on a fee for their services (normally $10/account enrolled or similar). You then pay them the large payment every month and they disperse it amongst your creditors for a specified amount of time (normally 3-5 years). Again, you can also negotiate for yourself without them. Mainly, just have a good idea and stay true to yourself of what you can afford on a monthly basis and just ask your creditor!

What if I cant afford these options?

You may need a professional's help in this case. I would consider speaking with an attorney to explore the possibility of bankruptcy and how that would affect your credit. Especially if you are drowning in medical debt. If bankruptcy is a little extreme in the moment, you may try working side hustles to help supplement your income or to at least afford groceries (You should always put yourself first!)
